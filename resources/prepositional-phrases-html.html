1. If my manager was away, I would …

  • put off anything that carries too much responsibility until they return.
  • feel confident in deciding which issues can wait and which genuinely need immediate attention.
  • fill in for them, making as many important decisions as possible so they have less to do on their return.

2. If I realised I’d made a mistake in a report, I would …

  • cover up the mistake and hope nobody notices.
  • own up to it immediately, even if it’s embarrassing.
  • accept that I’m responsible for fixing it, but only tell people if the mistake is likely to affect them.

3. By Friday afternoon, I usually …

  • feel capable of leaving work unfinished if I’ve focused on the right priorities.
  • fall behind on less urgent tasks because unexpected work keeps appearing.
  • catch up on everything that’s built up during the week.

4. If I promised to help a colleague, I would …

  • not feel responsible for solving other people’s problems unless it was part of my role.
  • back out of it if my own workload became too heavy.
  • follow through on my promise, even if it meant rearranging my own schedule.

5. When a project starts going wrong, I think it usually …

  • is dependent on so many different factors that it’s rarely possible to blame just one cause.
  • comes down to poor planning at the beginning.
  • stems from people avoiding difficult conversations.

6. After an important client meeting, I usually …

  • hand over the next steps to whoever owns that part of the project.
  • follow up on everything we’ve agreed before the end of the day.
  • stay involved in the relationship even after my own work is finished.

7. If my workload suddenly doubled, I would …

  • ask for support before the pressure started affecting the quality of my work.
  • take on the extra work without complaining.
  • stay focused on the tasks that would have the biggest impact and accept that some things would have to wait.

8. When I receive constructive criticism, I …

  • remain open to different opinions, but decide for myself which suggestions are worth following.
  • brush off feedback if I don’t think it’s fair.
  • act on it immediately and try to improve.

9. During team meetings, I usually …

  • hold back unless someone asks for my opinion directly.
  • feel comfortable with disagreeing respectfully if I think the discussion is heading in the wrong direction.
  • speak up whenever I think I have something valuable to add.

10. If my company introduced a major change, I would …

  • become curious about how the change might improve the way we work.
  • stick to my existing routine until I was forced to change.
  • get to grips with the new system as quickly as possible.

Phrasal Verbs and Dependent Prepositions

A Phrasal Verb combines a verb with one or two prepositions to create a meaning that is often quite different from the individual words.

For example:

  • Verb: to look (to turn your eyes towards something or someone so that you can see them)
  • Phrasal Verb: to look up (to find information in a book, on a computer, etc.)
  • Phrasal Verb: to look forward to (to be excited and happy about something that is going to happen)

A Dependent Preposition is a preposition that habitually follows a particular adjective or noun.

For example:

  • adjective + preposition — responsible for (“Sabrina is responsible for onboarding new staff.”)
  • noun + preposition — access to (“All employees have access to this database.”)
