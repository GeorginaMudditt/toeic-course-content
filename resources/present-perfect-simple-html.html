Present Perfect Simple

💡
Note importante :

En français, on utilise le passé composé pour parler d'actions passées qui ont un lien avec le présent (J'ai déjà fini mes devoirs).

En anglais :

Past Simple = action complètement terminée dans le passé (I finished my homework yesterday).

Present Perfect Simple = action passée ayant un résultat ou un lien avec le présent (I have finished my homework).

Grammar Point #1: Present Perfect Simple

Structure Examples
+ Subject + have/has + past participle I have visited Bristol.
She has read that book.
They have eaten lunch.
- Subject + haven't/hasn't + past participle I haven't visited London.
She hasn't read that book.
They haven't eaten lunch.
? Have/Has + subject + past participle? Have you visited London?
Has she read that book?
Have they eaten lunch?

Uses:

  • Actions that started in the past and continue to the present (e.g. "They have both worked here for five years.")
  • Changes over time (e.g. "The price of petrol has increased.")
  • Recent past actions which are important in the present (e.g. "She has just left the office.")
  • Experiences or accomplishments (e.g. "He has travelled to ten countries in Europe.")
  • Actions that happened at an unspecified time before now (e.g. "I have watched the training video.")
  • Unfinished time periods (e.g. "I have had four cups of coffee today.")

Notes:

  • Short answers: Yes, I have. / No, I haven't. / Yes, he has. / No, he hasn't.
  • These adverbs are often used with the Present Perfect: already, yet, just, ever, never
  • I have already finished preparing the presentation. (already = sooner than expected)
  • Have you booked the hotel yet? (yet = until now, used in questions and negatives)
  • They have just arrived. (just = a few moments ago)
  • Have you ever worked with this software? (ever = at any time)
  • I have never used this platform before. (never = at no time)
