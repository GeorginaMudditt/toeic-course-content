Ellipsis & Substitution

Grammar Point #1: What is ellipsis?

Ellipsis is the omission of words that are understood from context, so they don't need to be repeated. It is commonly used when speakers and writers want to be efficient and avoid repetition.

Full sentence:

The marketing team will present a proposal, and the finance team will present a proposal too.

With ellipsis:

The marketing team will present a proposal, and the finance team will ∅ too.

👉 The missing words (present a proposal) are understood from context.'∅' shows where some words have been removed.

Grammar Point #2: Types of ellipsis

A. Verb phrase ellipsis

We omit part of the verb phrase when it is repeated.

Toni has arrived at the hotel in Tokyo, and Ross ∅ too.

('∅' shows where some words have been removed. In this case those words are has arrived at the hotel in Tokyo)

The HR team will contact the candidates, and the legal team will ∅ as well.

(∅ = contact the candidates)

B. Ellipsis after auxiliaries

Common after: be, have, do, will, can, should, etc.

We can finalise the contract today, and they can ∅ tomorrow.

(∅ = finalise the contract)

The CEO has approved the plan, and the board has ∅ too.

(∅ = approved the plan)

C. Ellipsis in coordinated clauses

Sales increased in Europe, and profits ∅ in Asia.

(∅ = increased)

The manager reviewed the budget, and the director ∅ the strategy.

(∅ = reviewed)

D. Short responses

"Will you attend the call?" – "Yes, I will ∅."

(∅ = attend the call)

"Have you reviewed the figures?" – "Yes, I have ∅."

(∅ = reviewed the figures)

E. Nominal ellipsis (= ellipsis of nouns/objects)

I prefer the revised proposal, not the original ∅.

(∅ = proposal)

We need the updated version, not the previous ∅.

(∅ = version)

Grammar Point #3: What is substitution?

Substitution means replacing a word or phrase with a general substitute word to avoid repetition.

Unlike ellipsis, a word or phrase is substituted instead of being omitted.

A. Using "do / did / do so"

The team promised to deliver the project, and they did.

(did = delivered the project)

She said she would follow up, and she did so immediately.

(did so = followed up)

Note: "did" and "did so" (or "do" and "do so") can be used interchangeably. However, "did so" is slightly more formal than "did" alone. Both are grammatically correct and acceptable in formal writing.

B. Using "so / not"

The CEO expects growth this quarter, and the investors think so too.

(so = there will be growth this quarter)

They said the merger would succeed, but it seems not.

(not = it won't succeed)

C. Using "one / ones"

I prefer the updated strategy, not the previous one.

(one = strategy)

These figures are better than the ones from last quarter.

(ones = figures)
