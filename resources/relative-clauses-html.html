Relative Clauses

Introduction

Relative clauses give us more information about people, things, places, times, and reasons. They help us combine ideas and write more sophisticated sentences.

Two separate sentences: I work with a woman. She speaks five languages.

Combined with relative clause: I work with a woman who speaks five languages.

Grammar Focus #1: Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Defining Relative Clauses

These give essential information to identify which person/thing we mean. Without this information, the sentence doesn't make sense.

  • The woman who lives next door is a doctor. (Which woman? The one who lives next door)
  • I need a car that uses less petrol. (What kind of car? One that uses less petrol)
  • No commas are used
  • "That" can replace "who/which"

Non-Defining Relative Clauses

These give extra, non-essential information. The sentence makes sense without the clause.

  • My nephew, who is 19 years old, has gone to Australia for a year. (Extra information about my nephew)
  • Paris, where I grew up, is a beautiful city. (Extra information about Paris)
  • Commas are required (or dashes/brackets in writing)
  • "That" cannot be used

Practice #3: Omitting Relative Pronouns

Cross out the relative pronouns that can be omitted. If it cannot be omitted, write "necessary".

You can only omit the relative pronoun when it is the object of the relative clause. You cannot omit it when it's the subject.

SUBJECT = the prounoun does the action. For example, in "The man who called me ..." the man is the subject who is doing the action so "who" cannot be omitted.

OBJECT = the prounoun receives the action. For example, in "The book that Sam is reading ..." the book is the object receiving the action (and Sam is the subject doing the action) so "that" can be omitted.

Grammar Focus #3: Prepositions in Relative Clauses

Prepositions can come at the end (informal) or before the relative pronoun (formal).

Informal: The person who I spoke to was very helpful.

Formal: The person to whom I spoke was very helpful.

Informal: The house which we're looking at is too expensive.

Formal: The house at which we're looking is too expensive.

Note: After a preposition, use whom (not who) for people and which (not that) for things.

Grammar Focus #4: Reduced Relative Clauses

We can shorten relative clauses by:

1. Removing the relative pronoun + "be":

  • The people who are waiting outside ‚Üí The people waiting outside
  • The emails which were sent yesterday ‚Üí The emails sent yesterday

2. Using a present participle (-ing) for active meaning:

  • Anyone who wants to leave early ‚Üí Anyone wanting to leave early

3. Using a past participle (-ed) for passive meaning:

  • The report which was written by John ‚Üí The report written by John
