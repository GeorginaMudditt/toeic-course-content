The Subjunctive

Grammar Point #1 — What is the subjunctive?

The subjunctive is a verb mood used to express hypothetical situations, wishes, recommendations, demands, doubts, and emotional responses. It is not a tense; it is a mood, which means it expresses the speaker's attitude towards the action rather than the time of the action.

The subjunctive is most commonly found in formal or written contexts, at C1 level and above.

There are two main forms to know:

A. The Present Subjunctive

The present subjunctive uses the base form of the verb (infinitive without 'to') for all subjects, including third-person singular — meaning there is no '-s' ending.

Indicative (standard):

She works from home.

Subjunctive:

I suggest that she work from home.

Indicative:

He is on time.

Subjunctive:

It is essential that he be on time.

Key triggers (verbs of recommendation, demand, suggestion, requirement):

  • suggest, recommend, insist, demand, require, propose, request, urge
  • It is essential / vital / important / necessary / crucial that...

B. The Past Subjunctive

The past subjunctive uses 'were' for all persons (including I, he, she, it). It is used to talk about hypothetical or unreal situations in the present or future. We have already seen this structure in the Second Conditional.

Indicative:

I am not the manager.

Subjunctive:

If I were the manager, I would restructure the team.

Indicative:

She is here.

Subjunctive:

I wish she were here.

Key triggers:

  • If I were... / Were I to... (formal conditionals)
  • I wish... / If only...
  • as if / as though
  • It's (high) time...

Grammar Point #2 — Common structures

A. "that" clauses after verbs of recommendation/demand

Note: This is the most common use of the present subjunctive in formal English. Note that "that" can be omitted.

The board recommended (that) the CEO resign immediately.

Doctors insist (that) the patients avoid strenuous activity.

It is vital (that) every delegate submit their report by Friday.

The contract requires (that) the supplier deliver within 30 days.

B. Hypothetical conditionals with "were"

If I were you, I would think carefully before accepting.

If she were to resign, the company would face a serious crisis.

Were he to find out, there would be serious consequences. (formal inversion)

C. "I wish" and "If only" (present unreal wish)

I wish he were more reliable. (= He is not reliable)

If only she were here to see this. (= She is not here)

I wish I were able to attend the conference. (= I cannot attend the conference)

D. "As if" / "As though"

He speaks as if he were the director. (= He is not the director)

She acted as though nothing had happened. (= Something had happened)

E. "It's time" / "It's high time"

It's time we revised our approach. (= We haven't done so yet)

It's high time the government took action. (= The government have not taken action yet - criticism implied)

Practice #3 — Rewrite using the subjunctive

Rewrite each sentence so that it contains a subjunctive structure. Keep the meaning the same.

  1. The director told David he had to leave the meeting immediately.
    → The director insisted
  2. I'm not the Head of Department, but if I were, I'd change the policy.
    → Were I
  3. I regret that Carla isn't able to join us for the project.
    → I wish
  4. According to the terms, the vendor must notify us 30 days in advance.
    → The terms require that
  5. He seems to think he is in charge, but he isn't.
    → He behaves as if
  6. We really should have started this process a long time ago.
    → It's high time
