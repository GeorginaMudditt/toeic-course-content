Meetings

Speaking

Warm-up

  1. How much time in a week do you usually spend in meetings?
  2. What frustrates you about meetings? Is that the same for in-person and online meetings?
  3. What do you think makes a meeting effective?
  4. Do you ever lead meetings in English? If so, what's the most difficult part?
  5. What do you think could make you more confident or efficient leading meetings in English?

Language

Opening

  • "Thanks for joining, everyone."
  • "Let's get started."
  • "The aim of today's meeting is …"
  • "We're here to discuss …"
  • "I'd like to start by …"
  • "Is everyone ready to kick off?"

Managing

  • "Let's focus on …"
  • "Can we bring it back to …"
  • "Can you expand on that?"
  • "Could you be more specific?"
  • "Let's park that for now."
  • "Let's come back to that point later."
  • "We're running behind schedule."

Turn-taking

  • "What's your view on this?"
  • "Do you want to add anything?"
  • "Could we hear from …"
  • "I'd like to hear from … on this."
  • "Does anyone have a different perspective?"
  • "I'll hand over to … now."
  • "Just to jump in quickly …"
  • "Sorry to interrupt, but …"

Closing

  • "To sum up …"
  • "So, we've agreed that …"
  • "The next steps are …"
  • "Shall we wrap up?"
  • "We're running out of time."
  • "Just to clarify …"
  • "Let's follow up on this next time."

Phrasal verbs

You probably noticed that there are several phrasal verbs in the phrases above. Using phrasal verbs in your spoken English is a great way to elevate your level and show fluency.

Match each of the phrasal verbs to its meaning. Write the correct letter (A–J) in the box after each verb. Each letter is used once.

Definitions

  1. To begin or start something.
  2. To return to a previous topic or focus.
  3. To say more about something; to give more detail.
  4. To receive someone's opinion or contribution.
  5. To pass control or the floor to someone else.
  6. To enter a conversation, often interrupting.
  7. To give a brief summary of what has been said.
  8. To bring something to an end or conclusion.
  9. To have no more of something remaining.
  10. To do something as a next step after a previous action or meeting.

Role play

You are going to lead a meeting, in which your teacher or partner is a participant.

Choose one of the following scenarios:

  • You have collected some customer feedback or data. You want to present the data in a meeting and suggest some actions based on the feedback. The participants will decide on the priority and efficacy of the suggested actions.
  • You are launching a new product or service. You want to give some details about the product or service in an internal meeting and propose the strategy for the launch. The participants will work together to finalise this strategy.
  • You have several possible candidates for a job opening. You want to present the candidates in a meeting. The participants will decide who is best for the job.

Task

  • Open the meeting
  • Keep control of the discussion
  • Stay on topic
  • Reach a decision
  • Summarise

Feedback

  • What went well?
  • What could have been improved?
  • What did you find most difficult?
  • Did you feel in control?
