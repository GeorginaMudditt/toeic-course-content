Grammar Point #1: Present Perfect Continuous

Structure Examples
+ Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing - I have been studying.
- She has been working all morning.
- They have been playing football.
- Subject + haven't/hasn't + been + verb-ing - I haven't been studying.
- She hasn't been working.
- They haven't been playing football.
? Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing? - Have you been studying?
- Has she been working?
- Have they been playing football?

Uses:

  • Actions that started in the past and are still continuing now (e.g. "They have been working here for five years.")
  • Temporary actions or situations (e.g. "She has been staying with a friend while her flat is being renovated.")
  • Actions that have recently stopped but have a visible result now (e.g. "He is tired because he has been running.")
  • Repeated actions over a period of time, often to show irritation or emphasis (e.g. "They have been calling me all morning.")
  • Long or ongoing activities where the focus is on the duration of the action (e.g. "I have been studying for three hours.")
  • Actions with "for" and "since" to emphasise duration (e.g. "She has been living here since 2021.")

Notes:

  • Short answers: Yes, I have. / No, I haven't. / Yes, he has. / No, he hasn't.
  • The words for and since are often used with the Present Perfect Continuous:
  • I have been reading for two hours. (for = a length of time)
  • She has been living here since 2019. (since = a point in time)

Grammar Point #2: Quick Comparison

Present Perfect Simple Present Perfect Continuous
Focus on result (how much? how many?) Focus on activity / duration (how long?)
Completed tasks Ongoing or temporary actions

Example:

  • I have written three reports today. (result: 3 reports finished)
  • I have been writing reports all morning. (focus on activity/time)

Note: Sometimes the Present Perfect Simple and the Present Perfect Continuous can both be used with the same meaning.
