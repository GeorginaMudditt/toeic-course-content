Tenses

Present simple

  • • for habits (I drink coffee every morning)
  • • for general truths (Water boils at 100 degrees)
  • • for timetables (The train leaves at 8am)
➕

Positive

I live in the south of France.

He lives in Bristol in the south west of England.

Subject + base verb (+s for 3rd person singular)

➖

Negative

I do not (don't) drink coffee.

She does not (doesn't) drink tea.

Subject + do/does + not + base verb

❓

Question

Do you listen to music at the weekend?

Does he listen to the radio in the car?

Do/Does + subject + base verb + ?

Present continuous

  • • for actions happening now (I am writing an email)
  • • for temporary situations (I am living with my parents)
  • • for future arrangements (I am meeting John tomorrow)
  • • for changing situations (The weather is getting colder)
➕

Positive

We are (We're) waiting for the film to start.

He is (He's) waiting at the bus stop.

Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing

➖

Negative

I am (I'm) not enjoying this book.

She is (She's) not enjoying her meal.

Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing

❓

Question

Are you going to the party on Saturday?

Is he going to school on Monday?

Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing + ?

Attention !

En français vous utilisez le même temps pour les deux cas ci-dessus. En anglais vous devez faire la différence entre : quelque chose qui est toujours ou souvent vrai (present simple) et quelque chose qui se passe en ce moment (present continuous).

Past simple

  • • for completed actions in the past (I visited Paris last year)
  • • for past states or conditions (She was tired yesterday)
  • • for sequences of past events (He opened the door, walked in, and sat down)
➕

Positive

I went to bed late last night.

She went to the dentist yesterday.

Subject + past tense of verb (+ed or irregular)

➖

Negative

I did not (didn't) watch the football.

She did not (didn't) watch the film.

Subject + did + not + base verb

❓

Question

Did you work at the weekend?

Did he work on Tuesday evening?

Did + subject + base verb + ?

Past continuous

  • • for actions in progress at a specific time in the past (I was studying at 9pm last night)
  • • for background actions when something else happened (I was cooking when the phone rang)
  • • for two simultaneous past actions (While I was reading, she was watching TV)
  • • for temporary situations in the past (He was living in London at that time)
➕

Positive

I was sitting in the train station for a long time.

They were sitting in the park when it started to rain.

Subject + was/were + verb + ing

➖

Negative

She was not (wasn't) sleeping when I phoned her.

They were not (weren't) sleeping for very long.

Subject + was/were + not + verb + ing

❓

Question

What were you doing at midnight last night?

What was he doing when the accident happened?

Was/Were + subject + verb + ing + ?

Present perfect simple

  • • for past actions with present relevance (I have lost my keys - they're still lost now)
  • • for experiences up to now (She has visited Japan three times)
  • • for actions that started in the past and continue now (We have lived here for ten years)
➕

Positive

I have (I've) finished my dinner.

She has (She's) met some famous actors.

Subject + have/has + past participle

➖

Negative

We have not (haven't) had a chance to clean the house.

He has not (hasn't) applied for the job yet.

Subject + have/has + not + past participle

❓

Question

Have you ever been on television?

Has she fixed the leaking tap yet?

Have/Has + subject + past participle + ?

Present perfect continuous

  • • for actions that started in the past and are still continuing (I have been studying English for two years)
  • • for recent activities that explain a present situation (I'm tired because I have been working all day)
  • • to emphasise the duration of an ongoing activity (They have been waiting for three hours)
➕

Positive

I have (I've) been studying for my exams.

She has (She's) been working at the bank for ten years.

Subject + have/has + been + verb + ing

➖

Negative

She has not (hasn't) been paying attention.

They have not (haven't) been living together very long.

Subject + have/has + not + been + verb + ing

❓

Question

How long has it been raining?

Have you been waiting long?

Have/Has + subject + been + verb + ing + ?

Future : will

  • • for spontaneous decisions made at the moment of speaking (I'll help you with that)
  • • for predictions about the future (It will rain tomorrow)
  • • for promises and offers (I will call you later)
  • • for future facts and certainties (She will be 30 next year)
➕

Positive

He will (He'll) love Paris.

I will (I'll) pay you back tomorrow.

Subject + will + base verb

➖

Negative

I will not (won't) tell anybody.

They will not (won't) understand this grammar.

Subject + will + not + base verb

❓

Question

Will you phone me when it is ready?

What will you say to your dad?

Will + subject + base verb + ?

Future : going to

  • • for plans and intentions already decided (I'm going to visit my parents this weekend)
  • • for predictions based on present evidence (Look at those clouds - it's going to rain)
  • • for things that seem certain to happen soon (He's going to be late - he's still in bed)
  • • for arranged future events (We're going to have dinner at that new restaurant)
➕

Positive

Look! It is (It's) going to snow.

I am (I'm) sure we are going to win.

Subject + am/is/are + going to + base verb

➖

Negative

I am (I'm) not going to see my cousin tonight.

He is (He's) not going to get there on time.

Subject + am/is/are + not + going to + base verb

❓

Question

Are you going to make a salad?

Is he going to help you with your project?

Am/Is/Are + subject + going to + base verb + ?
