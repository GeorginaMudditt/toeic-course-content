Argumentation

Speaking

Warm-up

  • Do you enjoy debates or arguments? Why / why not?
  • What makes a strong argument?
  • Do you think you are a persuasive person?
  • How do you feel about compromise? Do you always have to be right, or are you comfortable changing your mind?
  • Have you had much experience arguing or debating in English?

Structure

A convincing argument generally follows this structure:

  1. Claim: State your position clearly upfront. What do you believe, and what are you asking the other person to accept?
  2. Evidence: Support your claim with facts, data, examples, or expert opinions. The stronger and more specific, the better.
  3. Reasoning: Explain why the evidence supports your claim. Don't assume the connection is obvious.
  4. Acknowledge counterarguments: Anticipate objections and address them. This shows intellectual honesty and makes your argument harder to dismiss.
  5. Conclusion: Restate your position. If relevant, call the listener to action.

Example

Here's a short example presentation following the model:

“The Case for Free University Education”

Claim

University education should be free for everyone. Access to higher education should be determined by ability and ambition, and not by your bank account or social class.

Evidence

In Germany, public universities charge no tuition fees to domestic or international students. Norway, Finland, and Denmark offer the same. Graduate employment rates in these countries remain high, and their economies are consistently ranked among the most competitive and innovative in the world.

Reasoning

This matters because it dismantles the most common objection to free education: that it lowers quality or economic output. These countries prove the opposite. When financial barriers are removed, a wider pool of talent enters higher education. Society gains more doctors, engineers, teachers, and researchers who might otherwise never have studied. The return on that investment far outweighs the cost.

Acknowledge counterarguments

Critics argue that free universities place an unfair burden on taxpayers, and this is a legitimate concern. However, the evidence suggests that a more educated workforce generates higher tax revenues over time, which offsets the initial investment.

Conclusion

Free university education is not an idealistic fantasy - it is a proven model that several successful nations already use. If we are serious about equality of opportunity and long-term economic growth, removing financial barriers to higher education is not a cost. It is an investment.

Practice

Decide on “True” or “False” for each of these statements. Your teacher will argue for the opposite point of view.

  • “Companies should monitor employees’ digital activity during work hours.”
  • “Internships should always be paid.”
  • “Work-life balance is a personal responsibility, not a company's.”
  • “Technology is making critical thinking weaker.”
  • “Soft skills are more important than technical skills.”
  • “Schools should teach emotional intelligence as a core subject.”
  • “Success is more about luck than hard work.”
  • “It is better to be realistic than optimistic.”
